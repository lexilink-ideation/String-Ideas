📖 Poem & Reading

Language as witness

"Shema"
Primo Levi, 1946
You who live secure In your warm houses, Who return at evening to find Hot food and friendly faces:
Consider whether this is a man, Who labours in the mud Who knows no peace Who fights for a crust of bread Who dies at a yes or a no.
Consider whether this is a woman, Without hair or name With no more strength to remember, Eyes empty and womb cold Like a frog in winter.

Language Focus

Click each item to explore how Levi uses language.

Imperative mood +
"Consider whether this is a man" — a direct command to the reader. Levi is not suggesting; he is ordering us to look. Who is he speaking to? Why does the command form feel so powerful here?
Dehumanisation +
"Without hair or name" — the Holocaust stripped people of every marker of identity: names, clothes, hair, family. How does removing a name also remove a person? Can you think of other historical examples?
Second person "you" +
Levi addresses the reader directly — "you who live secure." This makes the comfortable reader complicit. What responsibility does this create? Does being addressed as "you" make you feel accused, invited, or something else?
Contrast +
"Warm houses" vs "mud" — the poem is built on contrasts. Find all the opposing images. What does the gap between the two worlds reveal? Why is the contrast technique so effective in bearing witness?

The Holocaust targeted identity itself — who you were born as, what you believed, who you loved.
1933
Hitler becomes ChancellorNazi laws begin targeting Jews. Jews are dismissed from civil service jobs.
1935
Nuremberg LawsJews stripped of citizenship. Marriage between Jews and non-Jews made illegal.
1938
Kristallnacht"Night of Broken Glass" — Jewish businesses, homes, and synagogues attacked across Germany and Austria.
1941
Mass shootings beginEinsatzgruppen mobile killing units follow the army into the Soviet Union.
1942
Wannsee ConferenceNazi officials coordinate the "Final Solution" — systematic murder of all European Jews.
1945
Liberation and NurembergCamps are liberated by Allied forces. Nuremberg Trials begin holding perpetrators accountable.
Key concept: Genocide is not sudden — it is built step by step through laws, language, and silence.

Discussion: At which point on this timeline could ordinary people have acted differently?
Untermenschen
"Subhumans"
Nazi term for Jews, Roma, and Slavs. A single word designed to strip entire peoples of their humanity.
Endlösung
"Final Solution"
Bureaucratic language that hid mass murder behind technical terminology. The horror is invisible in the phrase.
Umsiedlung
"Resettlement"
The word used for deportation to death camps. It sounds administrative. It describes murder.
Sonderbehandlung
"Special treatment"
Nazi euphemism for execution. The gap between the word and its meaning is where genocide hides.
Dehumanising language makes violence psychologically possible for ordinary people.
Euphemisms allow perpetrators to follow orders without fully confronting what they are doing.
When language is corrupted, people lose the tools they need to object or resist.
This is why precise, honest language is a form of moral resistance.
1945–46
Nuremberg Trials
First international tribunal to hold individuals — not just states — accountable for war crimes and crimes against humanity. A landmark precedent in international law.
1948
UN Genocide Convention
The Holocaust directly led to the first international treaty defining and criminalising genocide. Raphael Lemkin, a Polish-Jewish lawyer, coined the word "genocide."
1952
German Reparations
West Germany agreed to pay reparations to Israel and to individual survivors — acknowledging financial responsibility alongside criminal accountability.
Ongoing
Memory & Education
Germany requires Holocaust education by law. Yad Vashem in Jerusalem preserves 4.8 million names. Memory is treated as a civic duty.

Vocabulary to review
genocide
The deliberate killing of a large group of people, especially those of a particular ethnic group or nation.
persecution
Hostility and ill-treatment, especially because of race, religion, or political beliefs.
dehumanisation
The process of depriving a person or group of human qualities, making them seem less than human.
testimony
A formal statement, especially one given in a court; a first-hand account of an experience.
accountability
The fact or condition of being responsible for your actions and being willing to accept consequences.
reparations
Compensation paid by a nation for damage or injury during a war or atrocity.
euphemism
A mild or indirect word used in place of one that might seem harsh or blunt — often used to hide the truth.
atrocity
An extremely wicked or cruel act, especially one involving physical violence or injury.
