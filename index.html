A twelve-session course weaving English language learning with transitional justice and the music of bearing witness.
Each session pairs a chapter of history with a cello work and a piece of literature — building English skills through testimony, vocabulary through real events, and writing through the most essential human question: how do we speak about what happened?
"When words fall short, music remembers." — Strings of Memory

Why the cello?

Of all instruments, the cello occupies the same register as the human voice. Its low resonance carries grief. Its singing upper strings carry hope. It does not shout — it speaks. And in speaking, it bears witness.

In each session, a cello work is chosen not as decoration but as emotional language — a way of approaching histories that resist easy words. Kol Nidrei. Barber's Adagio. Dvořák's concerto. Bach's unaccompanied suites. These are not illustrations. They are voices that say what testimony alone cannot.

Learning English through this music means learning to hear — and then to speak. The cello teaches us that what we feel can be expressed, even when we do not yet have the words. This course gives you the words.

What you build in English
Vocabulary in context
Each session introduces 6–8 words drawn directly from the history — genocide, testimony, reparations, ubuntu — learned not from a list but from the moment they mattered.
Reading real texts
Excerpts from Elie Wiesel, Maya Angelou, Nelson Mandela, Desmond Tutu. English as it is actually written by people who had something urgent to say.
Writing with purpose
Every session ends with a writing task — a paragraph responding to a real question. Not grammar exercises. Writing that asks you to think, take a position, and find your voice.
Speaking about difficult things
Discussion in English about history, justice, memory, and music. The hardest conversations are also the most useful practice — for language and for life.
